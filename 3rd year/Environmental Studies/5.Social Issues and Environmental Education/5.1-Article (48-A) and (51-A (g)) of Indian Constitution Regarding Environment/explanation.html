5.1 Article (48-A) and (51-A (g)) of Indian Constitution Regarding Environment

Article 48-A: Protection and Improvement of Environment

Article 48-A was added to the Indian Constitution under the 42nd Amendment Act, 1976. It is a Directive Principle of State Policy (DPSP), which means that it is a guideline for the government to follow while making laws and policies.

Text of Article 48-A: "The State shall endeavor to protect and improve the environment and to safeguard the forests and wildlife of the country."

Simple Explanation:

  • The government must take steps to protect the environment.
  • It should focus on preserving forests and wildlife.
  • Laws and policies should be made to improve environmental conditions.
  • Though this article is not directly enforceable in court, the government must consider it while framing laws.

Example:

  • The government launched the National Afforestation Programme to increase forest cover.
  • The Clean Ganga Mission was started to reduce river pollution.

Article 51-A (g): Fundamental Duty to Protect the Environment

Article 51-A (g) is part of Fundamental Duties, which means it applies to every Indian citizen. It was also added in the 42nd Amendment Act, 1976.

Text of Article 51-A (g): "It shall be the duty of every citizen of India to protect and improve the natural environment including forests, lakes, rivers, and wildlife, and to have compassion for living creatures."

Simple Explanation:

  • Every citizen must help in keeping the environment clean.
  • People should not harm forests, lakes, or wildlife.
  • We should be kind to animals and avoid cruelty towards them.

Example:

  • Not littering in public places and properly disposing of waste.
  • Participating in tree plantation drives.
  • Avoiding activities that harm animals, like poaching or illegal wildlife trade.

Environmental Protection and Prevention Acts in India

To ensure environmental protection, the Indian government has enacted several laws. Some important ones are:

  • The Environment (Protection) Act, 1986: This law was made after the Bhopal Gas Tragedy (1984). It gives the central government power to protect and improve the environment.
  • The Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1981: Aims to control air pollution by regulating emissions from industries and vehicles.
  • The Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974: Prevents water pollution by regulating the discharge of pollutants into water bodies.
  • The Wildlife Protection Act, 1972: Protects endangered species and their habitats.
  • The Forest Conservation Act, 1980: Prevents deforestation and ensures proper use of forest land.

CPCB and MPCB: Norms and Responsibilities

Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB)

The CPCB is a national-level body that monitors pollution and enforces environmental laws.

Responsibilities:

  • Monitoring air and water pollution levels.
  • Setting pollution control standards for industries.
  • Advising the government on environmental policies.

Example:

  • CPCB sets norms for vehicle emissions to reduce air pollution.

Maharashtra Pollution Control Board (MPCB)

MPCB is a state-level body that enforces environmental laws in Maharashtra.

Responsibilities:

  • Granting permissions for industrial projects based on environmental impact.
  • Conducting inspections and ensuring industries follow pollution control norms.
  • Running awareness campaigns about environmental issues.

Example:

  • MPCB took action against industries polluting Mumbai’s Mithi River.

Role of NGOs in Environmental Protection

Non-Governmental Organizations (NGOs) play a crucial role in protecting the environment.

Some Important Environmental NGOs in India:

  • Greenpeace India: Works on climate change and air pollution.
  • WWF India: Focuses on wildlife conservation.
  • Centre for Science and Environment (CSE): Conducts research on environmental policies.
  • PETA India: Works for animal rights and welfare.
  • Chipko Movement (Historic): A grassroots movement where villagers protected forests by hugging trees.

Examples of NGO Activities:

  • Save Aarey Movement: NGOs and citizens protested against tree-cutting in Mumbai’s Aarey forest.
  • Narmada Bachao Andolan: Opposed the displacement of people and environmental damage caused by large dams.
  • Swachh Bharat Abhiyan: Though a government initiative, many NGOs help in cleaning campaigns.

Conclusion

The Indian Constitution provides both government responsibility (Article 48-A) and citizen duty (Article 51-A (g)) to protect the environment.

  • Several laws and acts help in controlling pollution and protecting nature.
  • CPCB and MPCB ensure industries follow environmental regulations.
  • NGOs play a vital role in spreading awareness and working on conservation efforts.
  • By following these laws and responsibilities, we can ensure a cleaner and greener future for the next generations.
