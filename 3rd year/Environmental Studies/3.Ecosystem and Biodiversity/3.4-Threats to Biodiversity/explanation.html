3.4 Threats to Biodiversity

There are several factors that pose a danger to biodiversity. These threats can lead to species extinction, habitat loss, and ecosystem destruction. Some of the major threats include:

1. Habitat Destruction

The most significant threat to biodiversity is the destruction of natural habitats. Forests, wetlands, coral reefs, and grasslands are being cleared for agriculture, urbanization, and industrialization.

Example: The Amazon Rainforest is being cut down at an alarming rate to make space for cattle ranching and farming.

2. Pollution

Pollution from industries, agriculture, and households affects air, water, and soil quality, harming plants and animals.

Example: Plastic pollution in oceans kills marine animals like turtles, dolphins, and fish.

3. Climate Change

Global warming and unpredictable weather patterns affect ecosystems and species' survival. Rising temperatures cause ice caps to melt, endangering polar bears and penguins.

Example: Coral bleaching occurs when corals lose their color and die due to warmer ocean temperatures.

4. Overexploitation of Resources

Excessive hunting, fishing, and logging can lead to species decline.

Example: The overhunting of the dodo bird led to its extinction. Overfishing in oceans is reducing fish populations, impacting marine food chains.

5. Invasive Species

When non-native species are introduced to a new environment, they may outcompete native species and disrupt ecosystems.

Example: The introduction of water hyacinth in Indian lakes and rivers has led to water pollution and the suffocation of aquatic life.

6. Deforestation

Large-scale clearing of forests for timber, agriculture, and settlements leads to habitat loss and global warming.

Example: The Sundarbans mangrove forests in India and Bangladesh are shrinking due to excessive tree cutting.

7. Industrial and Agricultural Expansion

Chemical fertilizers and pesticides used in farming harm soil, water, and wildlife.

Example: Pesticides like DDT once harmed bird populations, including eagles and falcons, by weakening their eggshells.

Hotspots of Biodiversity

A biodiversity hotspot is a region that is rich in species diversity but is under severe threat due to human activities. To qualify as a biodiversity hotspot, a region must meet two criteria:

  • It must have at least 1,500 species of vascular plants as endemic species (species found nowhere else in the world).
  • It must have lost at least 70% of its original natural vegetation due to human activities.

Examples of Biodiversity Hotspots in the World

There are 36 biodiversity hotspots globally. Some important ones include:

  • Amazon Rainforest (South America) – The world’s largest tropical rainforest, home to jaguars, macaws, and thousands of plant species.
  • The Congo Basin (Africa) – A dense rainforest home to gorillas, chimpanzees, and rare plant species.
  • Sundaland (Southeast Asia) – Includes Indonesia, Malaysia, and surrounding areas with species like orangutans and tigers.
  • Madagascar (Africa) – An island with unique species such as lemurs found nowhere else.

Biodiversity Hotspots in India

India has four major biodiversity hotspots:

  • The Himalayas – Includes the entire Indian Himalayan region and parts of neighboring countries. It is home to snow leopards, red pandas, and rhododendrons.
  • The Indo-Burma Region – Covers northeastern India, Myanmar, and surrounding countries. It has many endangered species like the one-horned rhinoceros.
  • The Western Ghats – A mountain range along the west coast of India, home to species like the Malabar civet and Nilgiri tahr.
  • The Sundaland (Including the Nicobar Islands) – Home to exotic species like the Nicobar pigeon and saltwater crocodiles.

Why Should We Protect Biodiversity?

Biodiversity is crucial because it:

  • ✔️ Provides food, medicine, and resources for survival.
  • ✔️ Maintains ecological balance and supports all life forms.
  • ✔️ Helps in climate regulation and disaster prevention.

How Can We Protect Biodiversity?

  • Reduce deforestation and plant more trees.
  • Avoid plastic and reduce pollution.
  • Implement laws to protect endangered species.
  • Promote sustainable farming and fishing practices.
  • Raise awareness about the importance of biodiversity.

Conclusion

Biodiversity is essential for maintaining life on Earth, but it is under severe threat due to human activities. Protecting biodiversity hotspots and reducing environmental damage is necessary for a sustainable future. Through conservation efforts and responsible human actions, we can save our planet’s rich biodiversity.
