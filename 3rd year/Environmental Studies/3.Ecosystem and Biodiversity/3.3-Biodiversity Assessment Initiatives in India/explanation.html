3.3 Biodiversity Assessment Initiatives in India

Biodiversity refers to the variety of life on Earth, including plants, animals, and microorganisms, as well as the ecosystems they form. Assessing biodiversity means identifying and recording different species, understanding their distribution, and evaluating the health of ecosystems. In India, several initiatives have been taken to study and protect biodiversity.

Why is Biodiversity Assessment Important?

  • Conservation Planning – Helps in protecting endangered species.
  • Sustainable Development – Ensures that natural resources are used wisely.
  • Climate Change Mitigation – Helps in understanding how species react to environmental changes.
  • Economic Benefits – Many industries like medicine, agriculture, and tourism depend on biodiversity.

Major Biodiversity Assessment Initiatives in India

1. Indian Biodiversity Information System (IBIS)

A digital platform that collects and shares biodiversity data.

Scientists and researchers use it to track species and ecosystems.

Example: A scientist in Kerala can use IBIS to check records of rare bird sightings across India.

2. Biological Diversity Act (2002)

A legal framework for conserving biodiversity and ensuring fair use of biological resources.

Establishes National Biodiversity Authority (NBA) to oversee biodiversity conservation.

Example: If a pharmaceutical company wants to use a rare Himalayan herb, they need permission from the NBA to ensure sustainable use.

3. National Biodiversity Action Plan (NBAP)

A government policy that outlines steps to protect biodiversity.

Focuses on habitat conservation, species protection, and sustainable resource use.

Example: Under NBAP, India has developed plans to protect the Great Indian Bustard, one of the world’s most endangered birds.

4. Biodiversity Hotspot Identification

India has four biodiversity hotspots:

  • Himalayas (Snow leopards, Red pandas)
  • Indo-Burma region (Hoolock Gibbons, Assam roofed turtles)
  • Western Ghats (Lion-tailed macaques, Malabar civets)
  • Sundaland (Andaman & Nicobar Islands) (Nicobar megapodes, Dugongs)

Example: The Western Ghats are studied to monitor the health of frog populations, as they indicate climate change effects.

5. All India Coordinated Project on Taxonomy (AICOPTAX)

A government project that classifies and records plant and animal species.

Helps scientists identify new species and update old classifications.

Example: In 2021, a new species of snake called Trimeresurus salazar was discovered in Arunachal Pradesh under this initiative.

6. Indian State of Forest Report (ISFR)

A biennial (every two years) report that assesses forest cover and biodiversity.

Provides data on tree species, wildlife corridors, and deforestation rates.

Example: The 2023 ISFR reported an increase in mangrove forests along India’s coastline, which is good news for marine biodiversity.

7. National Mission for Green India (GIM)

A government program to increase forest and tree cover.

Focuses on restoring degraded ecosystems.

Example: In Rajasthan, the GIM has helped plant native trees to restore desert ecosystems.

8. People’s Biodiversity Registers (PBRs)

Community-based documentation of local biodiversity.

Villagers and tribal communities help record plant and animal species in their area.

Example: In Karnataka, villagers documented over 300 medicinal plants used in traditional healing practices.

9. Zoological Survey of India (ZSI) & Botanical Survey of India (BSI)

ZSI studies animal species; BSI studies plant species.

These organizations help in discovering and documenting new species.

Example: In 2020, ZSI identified a new species of freshwater fish in Meghalaya.

10. Wildlife Protection Act (1972)

Protects endangered animals and their habitats.

Helps in setting up National Parks and Wildlife Sanctuaries.

Example: The Act played a major role in saving the Royal Bengal Tiger through Project Tiger.

Conclusion

Biodiversity assessment initiatives in India play a crucial role in understanding and protecting the country’s rich natural heritage. With projects like IBIS, PBRs, and AICOPTAX, India continues to identify, document, and conserve its vast biodiversity. These initiatives not only help in conservation but also ensure sustainable development for future generations.
