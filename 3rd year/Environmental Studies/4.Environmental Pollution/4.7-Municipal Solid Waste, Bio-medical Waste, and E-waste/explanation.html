4.7 Municipal Solid Waste, Bio-medical Waste, and E-waste

Waste is any material that is no longer needed or useful. Different types of waste are generated daily from households, industries, hospitals, and offices. Proper waste management is essential to prevent pollution and health hazards. This section covers Municipal Solid Waste (MSW), Bio-medical Waste, and E-waste in detail.

1. MUNICIPAL SOLID WASTE (MSW)

Sources of Municipal Solid Waste

  • Household waste: Food scraps, plastic, glass, paper, old clothes, kitchen waste, etc.
  • Commercial waste: Waste from shops, offices, and restaurants (e.g., packaging materials, disposable plates, food waste).
  • Industrial waste: Leftovers from factories (e.g., paper, cardboard, scrap metals).
  • Construction and demolition waste: Bricks, cement, wood, and debris from construction sites.
  • Street waste: Leaves, dust, and litter collected from roads and public places.

Generation of Municipal Solid Waste

  • A single household generates 0.5 to 2 kg of waste per day.
  • Cities produce thousands of tons of waste daily.
  • Developed countries generate more waste compared to developing countries due to high consumption.

Characteristics of Municipal Solid Waste

  • Organic waste: Biodegradable (e.g., vegetable peels, food scraps).
  • Inorganic waste: Non-biodegradable (e.g., plastic, glass, metals).
  • Hazardous waste: Toxic substances (e.g., batteries, pesticides).
  • Recyclable waste: Paper, metals, and plastics that can be reused.

Effects of Municipal Solid Waste

  • Environmental pollution: Dumping waste in open areas causes soil, water, and air pollution.
  • Health hazards: Rotting garbage produces foul smells and attracts disease-spreading insects like flies and mosquitoes.
  • Clogging of drains: Plastic waste blocks drainage systems, leading to floods.
  • Greenhouse gas emission: Decomposing waste releases methane, a harmful gas that contributes to climate change.

Methods to Manage Municipal Solid Waste

  • Reduce, Reuse, Recycle (3Rs):
    • Reduce plastic usage.
    • Reuse items like glass bottles and old clothes.
    • Recycle paper, plastic, and metals.
  • Composting: Organic waste can be turned into compost for farming.
  • Incineration: Waste is burned at high temperatures to reduce volume and produce energy.
  • Landfilling: Non-recyclable waste is buried in landfills, but this should be minimized.

2. BIO-MEDICAL WASTE

Sources of Bio-medical Waste

  • Syringes, needles, and surgical instruments.
  • Used bandages, cotton, and gloves.
  • Expired medicines and chemical waste.
  • Human body parts (in rare cases, from surgeries).

Generation of Bio-medical Waste

  • Hospitals generate a large amount of waste every day.
  • A single hospital bed generates 1-2 kg of bio-medical waste daily.
  • Larger hospitals generate tons of waste per day.

Effects of Bio-medical Waste

  • Spread of diseases: Used syringes and blood-stained bandages can spread infections like HIV and Hepatitis.
  • Water contamination: If dumped improperly, bio-waste can pollute drinking water.
  • Air pollution: Incinerating bio-medical waste releases harmful gases.

Methods to Manage Bio-medical Waste

  • Segregation at Source: Waste is separated into different colored bins (e.g., red for plastic, yellow for infected items, blue for glass).
  • Disinfection: Used syringes and medical instruments are disinfected with chemicals before disposal.
  • Incineration: Infectious waste is burned at high temperatures in a controlled environment.
  • Deep Burial: Some waste is buried in designated pits to prevent contamination.

3. E-WASTE (ELECTRONIC WASTE)

Sources of E-waste

  • Computers, laptops, and mobile phones.
  • TVs, refrigerators, and washing machines.
  • Batteries, wires, and circuit boards.

Effects of E-waste

  • Soil and water pollution: Heavy metals seep into the soil and pollute groundwater.
  • Health risks: Toxic substances from e-waste can cause kidney and brain damage.
  • Air pollution: Burning e-waste releases harmful gases.

Methods to Manage E-waste

  • Recycling: Valuable metals are extracted and reused.
  • Proper Disposal: Special e-waste collection centers ensure safe disposal.
  • Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR): Companies are responsible for collecting and recycling their old products.
  • Refurbishing and Reusing: Old devices can be repaired and reused instead of being thrown away.

Conclusion

Proper waste management is necessary to protect the environment and human health. Every individual must contribute by reducing waste, segregating it properly, and following responsible disposal practices. Governments and industries should promote recycling and sustainable practices to reduce waste generation.

By managing waste efficiently, we can create a cleaner and healthier world for future generations.
